Thermal Analysis of a High-Power COB LED Array with Ducted Air Cooling for Horticultural Applications

Abstract

This study focuses on a thermal analysis of a high-power Chip-on-Board (COB) LED array, designed for horticultural applications. The primary objectives are to evaluate the effectiveness of the heat sink design in dissipating heat and to assess the overall thermal performance of the system under specified operating conditions. The analysis was conducted using SolidWorks 2023 Flow Simulation software. Two scenarios were investigated: an open-air configuration and an enclosed configuration with ducted airflow. The results indicate that the enclosed design provides more consistent and efficient heat dissipation.

1 Introduction

This report presents a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis of a novel horticultural lighting system based on a high-power COB LED array. The system is designed to provide optimal photon flux density and spectral uniformity for plant growth. A key feature of this system is its unique arrangement of COB LEDs, coupled with an advanced thermal management system and control system for optimizing light distribution. This study focuses on the thermal performance of the system, specifically evaluating the effectiveness of the heat sink design and the impact of ducted air cooling on heat dissipation.

2 System Description

The horticultural lighting system analyzed in this study utilizes an array of 61 Chip-on-Board (COB) LEDs. These COBs are arranged according to the centered square number sequence pattern (OEIS: A001844), which positions the LEDs in concentric square layers around a central lighting element. The purpose of this arrangement is to maximize uniformity in photon distribution when used in conjunction with a control system that allows for layer-wise intensity modulation, informed by a machine-learning optimization algorithm.

Each COB LED has a maximum power consumption of approximately 53 W. To ensure efficient heat transfer, a thermal interface material (TIM) with a thermal conductivity of 15.2 W/m/K (Frost X45 thermal paste) is used to mount each COB to a thin copper interface plate. This assembly is then directly mounted to the copper baseplate of a Scythe Fuma 3 CPU cooler, which is equipped with a Kaze Flex II PWM fan (25 mm).

The system also incorporates supplementary Surface Mount Device (SMD) LEDs (437nm blue, 660nm red, and 730nm infrared) disposed along the perimeters of the modular fixtures. These LEDs are separately controllable to achieve any desired total spectrum in conjunction with the white-based 3000K 90CRI COB LEDs, resulting in near-perfect spectral uniformity.

The COB LED array is designed for scalability and can be configured into larger rectangular arrays by placing two square arrays side-by-side with a linear connector module in the middle.

2.2 Thermal Management

The thermal management system is designed to maintain the COB junction temperature (Tj) in the low 30Â°C range under full load. The key components and design principles are:

  • Scythe Fuma 3 CPU Coolers: High-performance air coolers with four 6 mm copper heatpipes and a high-density aluminum fin array.
  • Frost X45 Thermal Paste: High thermal conductivity (15.2 W/m/K) for optimal heat transfer between the COB and the cooler.
  • Ducted Airflow: An enclosure with one high-static-pressure inline fan attached to an exhaust port, positioned diagonally across from the intake port, creating negative pressure to draw cool air across the heatsinks.
  • Bell-Mouth Intake Vents: Equipped with a dust filter, positioned diagonally opposite to the exhaust port.
  • Optimized Airflow: The ducted system ensures that all airflow is directed through the heatsinks, preventing hot air recirculation.

2.3 Control System

The lighting system features a control system that independently modulates the intensity of each concentric square layer of COBs. This layer-wise control, informed by a machine learning optimization algorithm, aims to maximize photon flux density uniformity across the illuminated area.

2.4 Lensing

Specialized lenses are affixed to the COBs to further enhance uniformity, especially for edge and corner COBs, which experience less beam overlap and are more affected by the reflective walls of the growing environment.

2.5 Supplementary SMD LEDs

The system is supplemented with Surface Mount Device (SMD) LED strips disposed along the perimeters of the frame. These strips are comprised of 437nm blue, 660nm red, and 730nm infrared LEDs, all separately controllable to emit any desired combination of spectra.

2.6 Modular Design

The COB LEDs are affixed to a modularized system consisting of four separate types of modular pieces, enabling infinite scalability across any size square or rectangular space. To form a rectangular arrangement, two square arrangements are placed side-by-side with a linear connector module disposed in the middle, resulting in a rectangular array with nearly equidistantly positioned COBs.

3.2 Boundary Conditions

Two scenarios were analyzed:

  • Scenario 1: Open-Air - The COB LED array was simulated without an enclosure, representing an open-air environment.
  • Scenario 2: Enclosed - The COB LED array was simulated within a heat-resistant plastic enclosure with a 250 mm diameter inlet and a 200 mm x 60 mm outlet. A 370 CFM exhaust fan was attached to the outlet.

In both scenarios, the Kaze Flex II PWM fans on the Scythe Fuma 3 coolers were operating at a maximum speed of 1400 rpm. Based on the results of the individual cooler analysis (Analysis 1), the initial temperature of the heat sinks was set to 42.5 Â°C.

3.3 Assumptions

  • The system operates under steady-state conditions after an initial transient period.
  • Heat generation in each COB LED is directly related to its input power and is assumed to be uniform across the COB surface.
  • Airflow is assumed to be laminar within the enclosure.
  • Heat transfer through radiation is considered negligible compared to convection and conduction.

5 Conclusion

This study analyzed the thermal performance of a high-power COB LED array designed for horticultural applications. Two scenarios were investigated: an open-air configuration and an enclosed configuration with ducted airflow. The CFD analysis, performed using SolidWorks 2023 Flow Simulation, revealed that the enclosed design provides a more consistent and efficient heat dissipation process compared to the open-air configuration.

In the open-air scenario, significant temperature gradients were observed, with higher temperatures concentrated in the central region of the array. This suggests that heat spreading is not optimal in this configuration. The non-uniform cooling rate indicates that heat dissipation efficiency is affected by factors such as uneven airflow distribution.

Conversely, the enclosed system demonstrated a more uniform temperature distribution, with an average temperature of approximately 33.33Â°C across the majority of the enclosure. The directed airflow facilitated by the enclosure and exhaust fan contributed to effective and stable heat dissipation.

While the enclosed design performs well, further improvements could be explored by optimizing the enclosure geometry to ensure a more uniform mass flow rate throughout the system, minimizing regions with slower airflow or higher temperature gradients. This could potentially be achieved by incorporating an additional intake vent and inline exhaust fan or modifying the enclosure design to improve airflow in the observed stagnation point. One such design modification could be to poke holes in the top of the heat-resistant plastic, to draw air through the top of the enclosure and spread it throughout the enclosed space.

Future work could involve investigating the impact of different fan speeds, inlet/outlet configurations, and hole positioning for the aforementioned design modification. Additionally, experimental validation of the CFD results would further enhance the understanding of the system's thermal behavior under real-world operating conditions.
